# Our Algorithmic Grey-Beige World

**Source:** [On my Om](https://om.co/2026/01/16/our-algorithmic-grey-beige-world/)  
**Date:** January 16, 2026  
**Category:** Essays, Technology

---

"The main purpose of my work is to provoke people into using their imagination. Most people spend their lives housed in dreary, grey-beige conformity, mortantly afraid of using colours." — Verner Panton.

"Most people are other people. Their thoughts are someone else's opinions, their lives a mimicry, their passions a quotation." — Oscar Wilde.

What Panton and Wilde couldn't anticipate was how technology would industrialize conformity. What used to require shame and ostracism is now baked into the internet's economic infrastructure. The algorithmic reality of technology platforms has codified conformity into the human condition. And it is very profitable.

Today, open YouTube and every single thumbnail looks the same. Shocked faces, specific color contrasts, carefully positioned text overlays. The algorithm rewards these patterns with distribution and punishes deviation with obscurity. Spotify has done the same with music. Songs engineered to be short, to provide an instant dopamine hit. Instagram and TikTok have scaled and gamified conformity to a whole new level. The grey-beige aesthetic is what gets distribution.

It was first noted in 2016 by Kyle Chayka: "AirSpace"—where Airbnbs, coffee shops, and co-working spaces across the world look identical. Silicon Valley amplified this blandness. Steve Jobs inspired black turtlenecks. Mark Zuckerberg jumpstarted grey hoodies. Allbirds became the unofficial shoe of tech. The mimicry was a simple signal: I belong to the winning template.

Algorithmic reality doesn't just commodify interaction. It standardizes imagination. We don't get unique. We get infinite variations of the same. Fall in line, and get paid.
